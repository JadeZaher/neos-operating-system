# precedent-challenge

## A. Structural Problem It Solves

Precedent without a challenge mechanism becomes dogma. Decisions made under one set of circumstances continue to bind the ecosystem long after those circumstances have changed, because there is no formal process to revisit them. Participants face a choice between silently ignoring outdated precedent (which erodes the memory system's credibility) or treating every past decision as permanent (which makes governance rigid and unresponsive). The precedent-challenge skill solves this by providing a structured, transparent process for challenging existing precedent. Challengers must demonstrate material grounds (changed circumstances, flawed rationale, harmful outcomes, conflicting precedent, or a better alternative), produce a written challenge brief, and submit it to the appropriate deciding body for review through the ACT process. The original record is never modified -- it is marked "overruled" with a link to the new decision. This preserves the full governance history while enabling evolution.

## B. Domain Scope

This skill applies to any decision record in governance memory, at any precedent level (routine, governance, or constitutional), from any domain and any layer. Any participant can initiate a challenge -- standing is universal, not limited to members of the domain that made the original decision. The deciding body for the challenge is the body that made the original decision, or its structural successor if the original body no longer exists. Constitutional-level precedent challenges require OSC consent. The skill covers: defining challenge grounds, writing challenge briefs, submitting challenges, the review process, and documenting outcomes (upheld, modified, or overruled). Out of scope: searching for precedent (see precedent-search), writing the initial decision record (see decision-record), and the ACT consent mechanics themselves (see act-consent-phase).

## C. Trigger Conditions

- A participant identifies an existing precedent whose rationale no longer applies due to changed circumstances
- A participant discovers a flaw in the original reasoning of a precedent that was not apparent at the time
- A precedent is producing harmful outcomes that conflict with ecosystem principles or active agreements
- Two existing precedents contradict each other and one must yield for governance coherence
- A demonstrably superior alternative to an existing precedent has been identified through experience or research
- A precedent search (per the precedent-search skill) surfaces a precedent that the searcher believes should be challenged

## D. Required Inputs

- **Precedent to challenge**: the specific decision record ID, with the holding and ratio decidendi quoted from the record
- **Challenge ground**: one or more of the defined grounds (changed circumstances, flawed rationale, harmful outcomes, conflicting precedent, better alternative) with evidence
- **Evidence**: documented support for the challenge -- data, changed conditions, identified flaws, or demonstrated harm
- **Proposed alternative**: what should replace the precedent if it is overruled (a new holding and rationale, or a modification to the existing holding)
- **Challenger identity**: who is filing the challenge (any active participant)
- **Precedent level of the challenged record**: determines the review body and process requirements

## E. Step-by-Step Process

1. **Identify the precedent.** The challenger uses the precedent-search skill to locate the decision record they intend to challenge. They read the full record, including the holding, ratio decidendi, obiter dicta, and dissent record, to understand the original decision's scope and reasoning. The challenger confirms the record is active (not already overruled or superseded).

2. **Select challenge ground.** The challenger selects one or more grounds from the defined list. **Changed circumstances**: the conditions that produced the original decision no longer exist -- the ecosystem has grown, structures have changed, or external conditions have shifted. **Flawed rationale**: the original reasoning contained an error that was not apparent at the time -- a factual assumption was wrong, a logical step was invalid, or information was missing. **Harmful outcomes**: the precedent is producing consequences that conflict with ecosystem principles, active agreements, or participant wellbeing. **Conflicting precedent**: two existing precedents contradict each other and cannot both stand. **Better alternative**: experience or research has revealed a demonstrably superior approach. The challenger must demonstrate a material basis, not merely disagreement with the outcome.

3. **Write the challenge brief.** The challenger produces a written challenge brief following `assets/challenge-brief-template.yaml`. The brief documents: the precedent being challenged (by decision record ID, with holding and ratio quoted), the specific ground for challenge with evidence, the proposed alternative (new holding and rationale), and the impact analysis (who is affected by the challenge and how). The brief must demonstrate that the challenge is substantive, not a relitigation of a settled question without new evidence.

4. **Submit the challenge.** The challenge brief is submitted to the body that originally made the decision. For routine-level precedent, the submitting domain's circle handles the challenge. For governance-level precedent, the original deciding body (or its successor) reviews. For constitutional-level precedent, the OSC reviews with a consensus process. If the original body no longer exists, the governance memory steward identifies the structural successor based on domain-mapping records.

5. **ACT review process.** The challenge enters an ACT process adapted for precedent review. **Advice phase**: those impacted by the precedent (identified from the decision record's affected_parties and the challenge brief's impact analysis) provide advice. The original participants in the precedent (if still active) have a particular perspective but no veto. **Consent phase**: the deciding body evaluates the challenge and determines the outcome: **Uphold** (the precedent stands, the challenge is dismissed with documented reasoning), **Modify** (the precedent's holding or scope is adjusted while its core reasoning is preserved), or **Overrule** (the precedent is replaced by the challenger's proposed alternative or a revised alternative that emerged during the ACT process).

6. **Document the outcome.** If the challenge is **upheld**, a challenge dismissal record is created as a decision record documenting: the challenged precedent ID, the grounds raised, the reasoning for dismissal, and the body's reaffirmation of the precedent. The original record is not modified. If the precedent is **modified**, a new decision record is created with the modified holding and ratio. The original record is marked "modified by [new record ID]" with a link. If the precedent is **overruled**, the overruling decision record must document: which precedent was overruled, why the original rationale no longer applies, and the new rationale. The original decision record is marked "overruled by [new record ID]" with a link. The original record is never deleted or edited -- the overruling annotation is appended.

7. **Apply semantic tags.** The outcome record (dismissal, modification, or overruling) receives semantic tags per the semantic-tagging skill. Tags include the challenged precedent's ID in related_precedents. The precedent_level of the new record is at least as high as the challenged precedent (overruling a governance-level precedent produces a governance-level record).

## F. Output Artifact

Either a challenge dismissal record (if the precedent is upheld) or an overruling/modification decision record (if the precedent is overruled or modified). Both follow the decision-record template with additional fields: challenged_precedent_id (the record being challenged), challenge_grounds (the grounds raised), challenge_outcome (upheld, modified, or overruled). The overruling record also includes: superseded_rationale (why the original reasoning no longer applies) and new_rationale (the replacement reasoning). All output artifacts are registered in governance memory through the decision-record skill's registration process. See `assets/challenge-brief-template.yaml` for the input artifact.

## G. Authority Boundary Check

Any active participant can initiate a challenge -- standing is universal. The deciding body is determined by the challenged precedent's level: routine challenges are decided by the domain's circle, governance challenges by the original deciding body or successor, and constitutional challenges by the OSC. The challenger has no special authority beyond initiating -- they participate in the ACT process as any other participant would. The deciding body cannot refuse to hear a formally submitted challenge with documented grounds; they must process it through ACT within 30 days of submission (14 days for routine, 30 days for governance, 60 days for constitutional). No individual can unilaterally dismiss a challenge. The governance memory steward ensures the process is followed but does not adjudicate challenges.

## H. Capture Resistance Check

**Capital capture.** Financial contributors cannot use challenges to overturn precedents that limit their influence. The challenge grounds must demonstrate material change, not merely financial preference. The ACT review includes advice from all affected parties, not just the challenger or funders. A donor who challenges a resource allocation precedent because it limits their spending authority must demonstrate changed circumstances, flawed rationale, or harmful outcomes -- not merely that the precedent is inconvenient for their financial plans.

**Charismatic capture.** A charismatic leader cannot use their social influence to ensure a challenge succeeds. The consent phase requires the deciding body to evaluate the challenge on its documented grounds, not on the challenger's personality. The challenge brief's written format ensures the argument stands on its own merits. If the same leader repeatedly challenges unfavorable precedents, the pattern of challenges is visible in governance memory and can itself be surfaced during advice phases.

**Emergency capture.** Crisis framing cannot be used to rush a precedent challenge through without proper review. Precedent challenges follow the standard ACT timeline, not emergency timelines, unless the challenged precedent is itself creating an emergency situation. If a challenge is genuinely urgent, the provisional emergency expediting rules apply, but the consent phase still requires all positions documented and dissent preserved.

**Informal capture.** Precedents cannot be informally overruled through practice drift -- participants ignoring a precedent does not overrule it. Only a formal challenge produces an overruling record. If participants notice that a precedent is being routinely ignored, this is grounds for a formal challenge (under "changed circumstances" or "better alternative"), not evidence that the precedent has already been overruled.

## I. Failure Containment Logic

- **Challenge brief is insufficient**: the deciding body can request additional evidence or clarification before proceeding to the consent phase. The challenge is not dismissed for procedural deficiency -- the challenger has 14 days to supplement their brief.
- **Deciding body does not process within timeline**: if the 30-day processing deadline passes, the governance memory steward escalates to the deciding body's parent circle or the OSC. The challenge remains open until processed.
- **Consent phase produces no clear outcome**: if the deciding body cannot reach consent on upheld, modified, or overruled, the challenge is escalated through the GAIA framework. At Level 4, a facilitator coaches the body toward a resolution. The precedent remains active during the challenge process.
- **Challenger withdraws**: the withdrawal is documented but does not prevent the deciding body from continuing the review if they believe the challenge has merit. Precedent health is a systemic concern, not solely the challenger's.
- **Simultaneous challenges**: if multiple challenges target the same precedent, they are consolidated into a single review process. All challenge briefs are presented together, and the deciding body evaluates all grounds simultaneously. The outcome addresses all challenges in a single decision record.

## J. Expiry / Review Condition

Challenge outcomes do not expire. A precedent that is upheld can be challenged again, but the new challenge must demonstrate different grounds or new evidence -- relitigation on the same grounds is not permitted unless the challenger can show that circumstances have changed since the previous challenge was heard. The challenge dismissal record serves as a reference point: subsequent challengers must distinguish their challenge from the previously dismissed one. Overruling records are subject to the same review schedule as any governance-level decision record. The precedent-challenge skill itself is reviewed when the governance memory steward identifies patterns that suggest the process is being used for capture (repeated frivolous challenges to delay governance) or is insufficiently accessible (valid challenges are not being filed because the process is too burdensome).

## K. Exit Compatibility Check

When a challenger exits during an active challenge process, the challenge remains open. The deciding body continues processing the challenge on its documented merits -- the challenge brief speaks for itself. If the exiting participant was the sole source of evidence, the deciding body notes this limitation and evaluates the challenge on available information. When a member of the deciding body exits, quorum rules apply per the act-consent-phase skill. If too many members have exited for quorum, the governance memory steward identifies the structural successor body. Challenge outcomes involving departed participants remain valid historical records. The departing participant's right to challenge expires with their active status, but challenges already submitted are processed to completion.

## L. Cross-Unit Interoperability Impact

A participant at one ETHOS can challenge a precedent established at another ETHOS if the precedent affects them (e.g., a cross-ETHOS resource allocation precedent). The deciding body is the body that made the original decision, regardless of which ETHOS the challenger belongs to. Cross-ETHOS challenges include participants from all affected units in the advice phase. When a precedent is overruled, the overruling record is visible across all ETHOS. If an ETHOS-specific precedent is overruled, only that ETHOS is directly affected, but other ETHOS may reference the overruling as persuasive precedent in their own governance. Cross-ecosystem precedent challenges (challenging a precedent in a different NEOS ecosystem) are not supported -- each ecosystem's governance memory is sovereign. Cross-ecosystem precedent influence is advisory only, handled through Layer V federation.

## OmniOne Walkthrough

OmniOne Co-creator Awa identifies a problem with a 2-year-old precedent on resource allocation thresholds. Decision record DR-OMNI-2024-018 established that any single resource allocation exceeding 10% of OmniOne's total resource pool requires OSC consent. At the time, the total pool was approximately $50,000, so the 10% threshold meant OSC involvement for allocations above $5,000. Since then, OmniOne has grown 5x -- the pool is now $250,000, meaning the 10% threshold requires OSC approval for allocations above $25,000. Awa believes this is disproportionate to the original intent.

**Step 1: Identify the precedent.** Awa searches governance memory using precedent-search and locates DR-OMNI-2024-018. She reads the full record. The holding states: "Resource allocations exceeding 10% of the total pool require OSC consent before disbursement." The ratio decidendi explains: "The 10% threshold ensures that significant financial commitments receive oversight from the ecosystem's highest governance body. This prevents any single circle from committing resources that materially affect the entire ecosystem without broad consent." The obiter dicta notes: "Several participants observed that the threshold may need adjustment as the ecosystem grows."

**Step 2: Select challenge ground.** Awa selects "changed circumstances." The ecosystem has grown 5x, transforming the practical effect of the 10% rule. What was a $5,000 threshold is now a $25,000 threshold. The original intent was to ensure OSC oversight for "significant" allocations -- but $25,000 may be significant at the same level as $5,000 was, while $5,000 allocations now bypass OSC entirely.

**Step 3: Write the challenge brief.** Awa produces a brief documenting: the precedent (DR-OMNI-2024-018, holding and ratio quoted), the ground (changed circumstances -- 5x growth), the evidence (pool growth from $50,000 to $250,000, allocation records showing the practical impact), and the proposed alternative: a tiered threshold system -- allocations above $5,000 require domain steward review, allocations above $15,000 require cross-circle review, and allocations above $25,000 require OSC consent. The brief includes impact analysis: all circles with resource allocation authority, the OSC, and the Economics circle steward.

**Step 4: Submit.** Awa submits the challenge brief to the OSC, the body that made the original decision. The governance memory steward confirms the OSC is the correct deciding body and logs the challenge submission.

**Step 5: ACT review.** The OSC opens the ACT process. During the advice phase, Economics circle steward Tomoko supports the challenge, noting that the current threshold creates a bottleneck -- the OSC processes 8-10 allocation approvals per month that the circles could handle themselves. Operations steward Marco has a concern: tiered thresholds add complexity. OSC member Kaito advises that the tiers should be defined as absolute amounts, not percentages, to avoid repeating the growth distortion problem. During the consent phase, the OSC reviews the challenge. Kaito proposes a modification to Awa's alternative: two tiers instead of three (domain steward review above $5,000, OSC consent above $20,000). No one raises a reasoned objection to this modified proposal. The OSC overrules DR-OMNI-2024-018 with the modified tiered threshold.

**Step 6: Document.** The overruling decision record DR-OMNI-2026-061 is created. It documents: challenged_precedent=DR-OMNI-2024-018, challenge_grounds=changed-circumstances, superseded_rationale="The percentage-based threshold produced disproportionate effects as the ecosystem grew 5x, defeating the original intent of the 10% rule," new_rationale="Absolute-amount tiers maintain oversight proportionality regardless of ecosystem size. The $5,000/$20,000 thresholds reflect the current scale." DR-OMNI-2024-018 is annotated: "Overruled by DR-OMNI-2026-061 -- tiered absolute-amount thresholds replace percentage-based threshold."

**Edge case: Simultaneous challenge.** While Awa's challenge is being processed, TH member Ravi files a separate challenge against the same precedent on "flawed rationale" grounds -- he argues that percentage-based thresholds were always conceptually wrong because they create moving targets. The governance memory steward consolidates the two challenges. Both challenge briefs are presented to the OSC during the advice phase. The OSC evaluates both grounds together. The overruling record DR-OMNI-2026-061 addresses both: "The percentage-based approach both produced disproportionate effects under changed circumstances (Awa's challenge) and was a structural design flaw that creates moving targets regardless of growth (Ravi's challenge). The tiered absolute-amount system resolves both concerns."

## Stress-Test Results

### 1. Capital Influx

A major donor challenges a precedent that caps individual financial contributions at 15% of the total resource pool. The donor argues "changed circumstances" because the ecosystem has grown and can absorb larger contributions. The precedent-challenge skill handles this without special treatment for the donor's financial status. The challenge brief must demonstrate material grounds -- the donor's financial interest is not itself a valid ground. During the ACT review, the advice phase surfaces concerns from participants who note that the contribution cap was designed to prevent capital capture, not to reflect the ecosystem's absorption capacity. The deciding body evaluates the challenge on its documented grounds, not on the donor's financial leverage. If the challenge is upheld (the cap remains), the dismissal record documents why the ecosystem reaffirmed the cap. If overruled, the new precedent must include capture resistance mechanisms that the original 15% cap was designed to provide. The challenge process is transparent -- all participants can see that a major donor challenged a contribution cap, ensuring community scrutiny.

### 2. Emergency Crisis

A natural disaster creates urgent resource needs that conflict with existing allocation precedents. A participant challenges the allocation precedent under emergency conditions, seeking to redirect funds. The precedent-challenge skill does not operate on emergency timelines for the challenge itself -- the challenge process follows standard ACT timelines. However, emergency resource reallocation can proceed through emergency governance authority (per Layer III emergency rules) without waiting for the precedent challenge to resolve. The emergency action is documented as an emergency decision record, and the precedent challenge proceeds in parallel. If the challenge succeeds, the new precedent retroactively justifies the emergency action. If the challenge fails, the emergency action stands as a one-time exception documented with emergency rationale. The key structural protection is that emergency conditions cannot be used to bypass the challenge process itself -- the challenge must still demonstrate material grounds and receive ACT review.

### 3. Leadership Charisma Capture

A charismatic OmniOne founder challenges a precedent that limits the OSC's authority to intervene in circle-level decisions. The founder argues "harmful outcomes" because, they claim, circle autonomy has led to poor decisions. The precedent-challenge skill resists charismatic capture because the challenge brief must document specific harmful outcomes with evidence, not general dissatisfaction based on the founder's authority. During the ACT advice phase, circle members provide their perspective on whether the precedent is actually harmful or whether the founder is seeking expanded authority. The consent phase requires the deciding body to evaluate the documented evidence, not the founder's personal credibility. If the founder's challenge is dismissed, the dismissal record documents why, creating a searchable precedent against authority expansion. The structured process ensures that the founder's social influence cannot substitute for substantive grounds.

### 4. High Conflict / Polarization

Two OmniOne factions fundamentally disagree about a resource allocation precedent. One faction challenges the precedent; the other defends it vigorously. The precedent-challenge skill handles polarization through the ACT process, which is specifically designed for navigating disagreement. If the consent phase cannot reach a result (too many reasoned objections on both sides), the GAIA escalation framework activates. At Level 3, a facilitator helps both factions articulate their core concerns. At Level 4, coaching helps the group explore third solutions that neither faction initially proposed. The challenge outcome (upheld, modified, or overruled) is documented with full rationale including the escalation path taken. The modified outcome may incorporate elements from both factions, creating a precedent that acknowledges the legitimate concerns of each side. The dissent record preserves minority positions for future reference, ensuring that the losing faction's arguments are part of governance memory.

### 5. Large-Scale Replication

OmniOne scales to 5,000 participants across 15 SHUR locations. Precedent challenges increase proportionally, but the skill scales because challenges are processed by the body that made the original decision, not by a centralized challenge court. A kitchen agreement precedent at SHUR Costa Rica is challenged by a SHUR Costa Rica member and reviewed by the local circle. A cross-ETHOS precedent is challenged and reviewed by the cross-ETHOS body. Constitutional challenges remain with the OSC. The governance memory steward circle (scaled from a single steward) manages challenge logistics: routing challenges to the correct deciding body, consolidating simultaneous challenges, and ensuring processing timelines are met. The precedent-search skill enables challengers at any location to find relevant precedents from any other location, and successful challenges at one location serve as persuasive precedent for similar challenges elsewhere.

### 6. External Legal Pressure

A government regulator identifies an OmniOne precedent that conflicts with new labor regulations and demands it be changed. The precedent-challenge skill processes this through the normal governance channel -- no external authority can directly overrule an internal precedent. A participant files a challenge on "changed circumstances" grounds (new legal requirement), the challenge enters the ACT process, and the deciding body evaluates whether the precedent should be modified to comply with the regulation while preserving governance integrity. The challenge brief documents the regulatory requirement as evidence of changed circumstances. The overruling record (if the challenge succeeds) documents the external regulatory driver, enabling future participants to distinguish between self-governed precedent changes and externally driven ones. The UAF sovereignty principle means the ecosystem processes external pressure through its own governance structure, maintaining institutional autonomy even while complying with legal requirements.

### 7. Sudden Exit of 30% of Participants

Fifteen of fifty OmniOne members depart. Several active precedent challenges involve departed members as challengers, deciding body members, or affected parties. The precedent-challenge skill handles this because challenges are evaluated on documented merits (the challenge brief), not on the challenger's continued presence. If the deciding body loses quorum, the governance memory steward identifies successor members or escalates to the parent circle. Open challenges by departed members continue to completion -- the brief speaks for itself. If departing members were key witnesses or evidence sources, the deciding body notes this limitation in their evaluation. The mass departure itself may trigger new challenges -- a participant might challenge precedents that were established by the departed majority on "changed circumstances" grounds (the community composition has fundamentally changed). These new challenges are processed normally, and the governance memory documents the relationship between the departure event and the resulting precedent evolution.
